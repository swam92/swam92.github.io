Scamazon

I've recently uncovered what I believe to be a massive scam- Proferred by the massive technology syndicate Amazon against we the people. It all started after deciding to watch the critically aclaimed, sordid television drama the Sopranos. The Sopranos explores the nuances of interpersonal relationships in the backdrop of a brutal criminal conspiracy, which, along with his family, Tony Soprano seeks to exert an element of control in an increasingly chaotic world. After exhausting the "free" areas of the usual suspects- Netflix, Hulu, Amazon, and Apple/ we realized we'd need to rent or buy the show. Netflix and Hulu didn't have it so we checked over on Amazon Prime Video. At the time the first season cost $10 to rent. Now it costs $25 to buy, without a rental option, but that doesn't diminish what is coming next.

So next we checked Apple TV. It was listed for sale, with no rental option, for $125. So I did the math. At $10 a season for 6 seasons thats $60. Apple was selling the whole series for $125. Thats over 200% more expensive. A pretty giant price difference. Ironically a debate about the ethics of Amazon and Apple ensued. Which company was more ethical? If it was Apple would it be worth it to pay over 200% more to reward their only relatively ethical behavior? In my mind it was laughable that such a dramatic price difference would ever justify whatever ethical incentive there might be to put dollars towards Apple instead of Amazon. And such a giant price different between two competiting companies didn't make sense. Something just didn't make sense.

So we decided that because Amazon didn't offer the show as a series, we'd check the price of each season. After a quick minute of clicking through a dumb smart tv interface on a remote, the answer unveiled itself. Amazon was charging $60 to rent the last season of the Sopranos. That would make the cost of watching the show, with only rental permissions, was $110. And AppleTV was charging $125 for the series. It was the clue we've been looking for!

I personally found this to be extremely deceptive. If you chose Amazon because of the price, and you invested yourself into 5 seasons of 13 hour long intense tv drama, no way you're going to be able to turn that down even at cost. And you wouldn't be able to buy the individual seasons from anywhere else, at least anywhere I stream from. So you'd have to pay the $50 you paid to watch the first 5 seasons for the final season! Gotcha! On balance I decided to definitely buy the show from Apple not only because I would own it for an extra $15, but because thats such a scammy thing for Amazon to do and for $15 I'll support an honest communication of price. It definitely seemed like it was a well-hidden price gouge. I just checked to grab pictures for this post and now Amazon is selling for the first 5 seasons for $25 each and the last season for $50. So the prices are comparable now, but the scam still stands, but now you'd just end up spending $25 that you thought you didn't have to IF you chose Amazon over Apple to buy the Sopranos.

June 6, 2020

On June 3rd 2020 I would have used the following words to describe my feelings towards the police: Reform. Regulation. Reason. My digital trail from that day includes phrases like: “I don’t think we should defund the police” and “the police are hopefully working to protect the protestors.” However, the wanton violence and excessive force I saw the evening of June 3rd is seared into my memory; it has irrevocably and changed my opinion about the state of law enforcement in America.

On that Wednesday night, I joined a protest walking through Central Park toward the Upper East Side, heading for Gracie Mansion, the official residence of the mayor of New York City. The entire park near the Mansion was barricaded by police, reaching it was impossible. After a powerful 8 minute silence among thousands of people, we moved south down York. The protest remained completely peaceful, and we occasionally stopped to take a knee.

Just before 9pm, an hour after the city-wide curfew took effect, at 60th and 3rd Avenue, I began seeing sirens at the back of the protest, about 3-5 blocks behind the front of the group, where I was located. We continued walking south, where I noticed the west side of 51st street, home of the 17th precinct, was fortified with barriers and police with firearms. Less than a minute later, numerous police vehicles, riot police, and bicycle and motorcycle mounted police swarmed the front - and as I later learned the rear* - of the protest. The police charged into our unarmed contingent of soft flesh in full body armor, swinging their batons indiscriminately and with impunity. The crowd of two thousand people – peaceful and unarmed – was now boxed in and being attacked by police on three sides. The police began amassing more units to block crosstown blocks and build a larger perimeter, a tactic known as “kettling”. The feeling in the crowd shifted from unity and solidarity to sheer terror as claustrophobia and panic set in. I can remember the look of panic in people’s faces as police crashed into us, violently beating those unfortunate enough to be caught in the front; the message this sent was loud and clear.

I managed to escape on my bicycle east on 50th Street to 2nd Avenue, and I continued riding south, where I saw more police vehicles en-route to the organized assault I had just left. I cut west on 45th Street, five blocks south of the police line, thinking I was now safe. As I approached the intersection of 45th and Lexington, a man yelled at me and another cyclist, “THEY’RE HERE! THEY’RE HERE! FIND ANOTHER WAY.” As I rode through the intersection, I saw several bikes strewn in the street. Groups of police tackled cyclists that were heading southbound. I could not believe my eyes. At this point there were police visible north, south, east, and west of my location. I froze, not knowing where to go. Another cyclist I was with was also panicking. “We’ve got to go, NOW!” he said. We decided to continue heading west. I had another 50 blocks home, in torrential rain. The police were everywhere. I felt a pit in my stomach each time the red and blue lights flickered around me. It was chaos. It was just dumb luck I made it home that night.

Many of my friends were not so lucky, they were arrested and brutalized by the police. The media covered the story, but did not paint the full picture. In an official statement Chief Monahan said that the police warned us to go home before making arrests. In the same statement, Monahan also appealed to looting as a justification for his attack, despite no reports of looting on the entire island at the time the assault. Chief Monahan was a man I respected after I witnessed him take a knee with protestors at Washington Square Park the day before. Unfortunately I am no longer of the same opinion after seeing his vicious, coordinated attack on innocent people, and his subsequent lies to the public. The news did not cover the police tackling people off of their bikes five blocks away from the protest. It was heartbreaking to see pictures and videos of the people I was just with being beat with batons and laying facedown on the street. These were people who had their hands up, evidenced from the picture below, which was taken moments before the attack:

Look closely at what the police are wearing. Here is an article that covers what happened at 50th Street and 3rd avenue that night. Watch the videos from here.

This occurred an hour after the 8pm curfew. Some may think that is a justification for the violence, as we knew that we were in violation. But the curfew is a scare tactic that empowers the police, criminalizes protest, and is being used as a reason to attack people indiscriminately. Things like the curfew are the type of action that need to be resisted- these police-state scare tactics are only being deployed to mute people’s voices and literally beat down pleas for the police to stop their violence. Most disturbing is the curfew has little to do with “law and order.” I am a resident of the SoHo neighborhood, an upscale shopping district where many of you have probably seen videos of large groups of people looting stores, starting fires, and causing mayhem. I witnessed this firsthand; I saw the police watch from across the street as my neighborhood was destroyed. I watched my neighborhood devastated by looters with impunity. Where were the police then? Why did the police do nothing to stop this? I watched over ten officers stand around a small trash fire talking and laughing for over 15 minutes while glass shattering echoed around the corner. Why did I watch the police allow my neighborhood to be looted, and two days later watch them violently assault a peaceful protest?

I can only speculate. Maybe they wanted to make the mayor look bad. Or maybe, they wanted to let those videos of destruction and chaos surface to sway public opinion and justify their subsequent use of force on peaceful, innocent people.

My views on the police have completely changed over the last few days. I have little respect left for the NYPD. The above is just my experience, which happened over the course of a mere three hours that felt like days. I think it speaks to the violence black people and other minorities face in this country daily. I can not imagine the terror some of our fellow citizens live with on a daily basis. My first-hand experience made me feel this pain and anguish more than any newspaper or television broadcast ever could have. Police are a danger to millions of people in this country. To the most vulnerable, they act as a hostile, occupying force. Their choice to attack protestors while turning a blind eye to looters leads me to believe they have a political agenda that is not in the interest of the people. They are a weaponized force that is designed to defend their monopoly on violence at all costs. They do not want to disarm. They do not want to be held accountable. We need major defunding of police, massive police reform, and civilian reviews with real power to keep citizens of the US safe.

*The rear was attacked by the NYPD's Strategic Response Group; a heavily armed, rapid response force for handling terrorist and similar emergency-like situations

Another thing I've been doing is studying for the FAA Private Pilot Knowledge Test. This is one of the steps, in addition to a medical exam, flight lessons, and a practical test, that is required to obtain a pilots license. Flying is something I have always loved, and getting a pilot license is something I have been considering for the last 4-5 years. I was originally going to go for a helicoptor license but I decided theyre too dangerous, impractical, and expensive. I am super excited about getting into aviation and learning to fly; this is the beginning of a hobby I hope I can maintain throughout my life. There are two types of licenses I am interested in: Sports Pilot and Private Pilot. A sport pilot requires less flight hours, in addition to being cheaper, so I may start with that upgrade when I have the time and resources. That being said, condensing your training schedule is the most efficient way to complete the lessons, because it will be easier to remember something you learned 2 days ago vs something you learned 2 months ago. It is left up to the instructor how many hours a student needs on a case-by-case basis. If you spread out those twenty hours over say, 12 months, the instructor may very well make you fly more hours in order to refresh and make sure you have the hang of everything.     USNS Comfort

    As many of you know, especially you who live in New York, the Mercy-class hospital ship USNS Comfort docked in New York City yesterday (March 30th). This behemoth of a ship was originally an oil tanker, before being converted into a hospital ship in 1976. The boat is designed to supply the Marines, Army, Air Force, and Navy with rapid and mobile medical and surgical capabilities. Crewed by about 1300 personnel, including about 1,150 medical professionals, the boat provides about 1000 hospital beds for patients. The ship is unarmed; if the ship were to carry offensive weapons it would be considered a war crime.

    So the USNS Comfort has been moved to New York to augment the capacity of the city's hospital systems as coronavirus patients take up an increasingly large proportion of hospital services. The ship is going to be used to treat non COVID-19 patients to relieve the burden on hospitals. But from my perspective on the cushioned armchair, it seems that one infected person getting on the ship will undermine that whole mission. Lets take a look at the numbers that I have used to come to this conclusion:

    • The most conservative estimate of COVID-19 patients, which is the official reported number of cases, is about 80,000 New Yorkers. We know realistically that for every reported case, there are about 10 that go unreported, so the real number is much higher. 45,000 of those cases are in NYC. But lets use the 45,000 number to represent the number of sick people in NYC.
    • The ship will hold 1300 personnel and 1000 patients. So lets use 2,300 as the total number of people who will be on the boat. Again, this is a conservative estimate because patients and doctors onboard will rotate, making the number of unique visitors greater.
    • The population of New York City is 8,600,000 million people.
    • So lets use division to get the ratio of COVID-19 patients to non COVID-19 patients (using the most conservative of estimates) : 45,000 / 8,600,000 = 0.00523
    • Finally, lets multiply the ratio by the number of people on the ship : (2300 * 0.00523) = 12.

    So these numbers lead me to believe that statistically speaking, if the ship is filled to capacity on day 1, 12 of those people will have COVID-19. It then seems like the virus will be liable to spread quickly throughout the ship given that space is generally tight on any boat, and cruise ships have proven to be a very dangerous place during this pandemic. The thing is once people on the boat become sick, the whole operation is compromised. Could you justify sending medical personnel and patients onto a boat where the virus is known to be? If not- everyone would have to evacuated. It would seem that it would make the most sense to designate the ship as a COVID-19 hospital, give the staff the proper PPE to operate it as such, and relieve the worry about the ship becoming a contamination zone. There might be a reason for not doing this that is as simple as 'the ship is not prepared to handle a scenario like COVID-19 and so cannot commit to doing so'.

    Maybe theres flaws in my assumptions and logic..if there is please feel free to point them out. I just became fascinated with the boat because it is huge and has been used as a field hospital all over the world; so this was on my mind.     Cats

    Katie and I have a full-time cat at home we call Bear. Katie has had bear for close to seven years now. But in addition to bear, we also foster cats, and sometimes kittens, from Best Friends Animal Society in Manhattan (BFA). BFA needs fosters for several reasons. When BFA recieves a cat from animal control (which is where all of their cats come from), it must be kept away from other cats for a minimum of ten days as to not potentially pass on any diseases it may have. Additionally sometimes the cats have known issues, and so foster parents take care of the cats, which often includes trips to the vet and administering medicine, until the cat get well and can be adopted out. As fosters we do not handle any of the logistics for adoption, besides returning the cat(s) to BFA. Additionally BFA gets many kittens so small that they need to be specially cared for (kept in a sterile kitten room) and bottle fed. When kittens get big enough to leave the kitten room, but not big enough to be spayed/neutered, which is a requirement for adoption, they go to foster parents. The foster parents will make sure the kittens are growing at the correct rate, take them to any necessary vet appointments including their spay/neuter surgeries, and then return the kittens to BFA where they will be adopted.
